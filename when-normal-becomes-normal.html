On Adaptation & Identity

When Does Normal
Become the Normal?

The quiet threshold where the strange becomes familiar — and why crossing it changes us more than we ever notice.

Essay Reflections on habit, culture & the self
·
Reading time ~ 8 minutes
·
Theme Philosophy · Psychology

There is a moment — quiet, unremarkable, almost invisible — when something that once felt strange stops announcing itself as strange. The alarm at 5 a.m. that used to feel violent begins to feel routine. The new city that once felt foreign begins to feel like home. The grief that once felt unspeakable becomes, with time, something one learns to carry without dropping. Nobody sends a notification. No line is crossed with fanfare. The new normal simply moves in, unpacks its things, and makes itself comfortable.

"We do not notice the fish tank change color. We are the fish. We are also the water."

— On habituation

But what, exactly, is happening when this shift occurs? And should we welcome it — or watch it with a careful, suspicious eye?

The Machinery of Habituation

Neuroscience has a clean word for this process: habituation. The brain, that relentless efficiency engine, stops firing loudly at stimuli it has seen before. Novel inputs generate prediction errors — spikes of neural activity that demand attention. Familiar inputs generate almost nothing at all. The brain says, in effect: I know this. Move on.

This is not a bug. It is the deepest feature of a mind built to survive in a dynamic world. Without habituation, every heartbeat, every breath of wind, every hum of the refrigerator would require conscious processing. We would be paralyzed by the ordinary. The brain's genius is precisely its capacity to absorb the world into the background, freeing attention for what is genuinely new.

The Mechanism

Psychologists describe this as hedonic adaptation — our emotional responses return to a baseline level after positive or negative life events. A promotion, a loss, a relocation: the peak of feeling fades, and what was once exceptional becomes the new floor of ordinary.

But here is the unsettling corollary: if the brain normalizes the good, it also normalizes the bad. Oppressive regimes have always understood this. Boiling frogs do not jump out of slowly heating water — the temperature rise is too gradual to trigger alarm. The same mechanism that makes city noise disappear into white sound also makes injustice, dysfunction, and mediocrity fade from conscious view.

Three Thresholds of Normalcy

I. The Personal Threshold — When a habit stops being a choice

The first threshold is intimate and private. Think of how you picked up a habit — a morning routine, a way of speaking, a style of thinking. At first it was deliberate: you were constructing something. Then it became automatic: it was constructing you. The behavior that began as will became character. The character that began as effort became identity.

This is how we become who we are. And it is precisely why the question of what we normalize matters so urgently. Because once something is deeply habitual, it is no longer evaluated. It simply is. The person who grew up in a home where anger was currency does not experience anger as unusual — it is the water they swim in. The entrepreneur who has normalized 80-hour weeks does not feel exhausted as an alarm; exhaustion has become the baseline against which they measure all other states.

The Personal Question

Ask yourself: what did I once find uncomfortable that I no longer notice? The answer is the cartography of your adapted self — both your resilience and your blind spots.

II. The Social Threshold — When a culture stops questioning its assumptions

The second threshold is collective. Cultures, like individuals, habituate. They have baseline assumptions so deeply embedded that they do not register as assumptions at all — they register as reality. A society that has normalized a certain distribution of wealth, a certain relationship with nature, a certain tolerance for cruelty, does not see these as choices. It sees them as facts.

Anthropologists call these invisible axioms the doxa of a culture — from the Greek for "belief." Pierre Bourdieu described them as the rules of the game that are so fundamental nobody remembers they are rules. The fish does not notice the water. We do not notice the fish tank change color. We are the fish. We are also the water.

History's moral revolutions — the abolition of slavery, the recognition of women's suffrage, the dismantling of caste hierarchies — were, at their core, acts of collective un-normalization. Someone, somewhere, looked at what everyone had stopped seeing and said: this is not inevitable. This is a choice we are making, and we can make a different one.

III. The Temporal Threshold — When emergency becomes permanence

The third threshold is historical and perhaps the most dangerous. This is the normalization of emergency — the moment when a crisis that was supposed to be temporary becomes the permanent architecture of everyday life.

Pandemic years taught many of us this lesson viscerally. Measures introduced as extraordinary — masks, isolation, surveillance of bodies — lasted far longer than emergency logic required, not because anyone decided to extend them consciously, but because the bureaucratic and psychological inertia of the new normal had already done its work. Emergency powers, once granted, are rarely returned voluntarily. Surveillance infrastructure, once built, rarely dismantles itself.

The same pattern plays out in personal life. The temporary workaround becomes the permanent system. The provisional arrangement becomes the binding contract. We meant to fix it later. Later became never. Never became just the way things are.

The Ethics of Normalcy

To normalize is not, in itself, neutral. It is an ethical act — an implicit endorsement. When we allow something to become normal, we are voting, through our silence and adaptation, to keep it. This is why the question of what we normalize is not merely psychological but deeply moral.

There is a strand of ethical thought — running from Simone Weil through Iris Murdoch — that locates moral failure not primarily in dramatic acts of wrongdoing but in failures of attention. We become complicit in cruelty not by choosing it, but by ceasing to look at it clearly. By letting it drift into the background. By letting it become normal.

"The opposite of love is not hate — it is indifference. And indifference is what normalization produces."

— After Elie Wiesel

The philosopher Avishai Margalit distinguished between a decent society — one that does not humiliate its members — and a civilized society — one whose members do not humiliate each other. Decency is a structural achievement. Civilization is a daily practice. Both require active maintenance, because both are vulnerable to normalization. The humiliation that becomes routine ceases to register as humiliation. It becomes policy. It becomes culture. It becomes just the way things are here.

Learning to See Again

So how do we guard against the colonization of our attention by the merely customary? How do we keep seeing clearly what repeated exposure has taught us not to see?

One answer is travel — not tourism, but genuine exposure to radically different ways of organizing human life. When you live in a society where the workday ends at 3 p.m. and the evening belongs to family and rest, the 70-hour work week you left behind stops being inevitable. When you encounter a culture where the elderly are woven into the fabric of daily life, the isolation of the aged in your home society stops being natural. Contrast is the solvent that dissolves false necessity.

Another answer is history. History shows us that virtually everything we take for granted was once unimaginable, and virtually everything that once seemed permanent eventually ended. The present is always more contingent than it feels. The normal is always more constructed than it appears.

A third answer — perhaps the most demanding — is what the contemplative traditions have always called wakefulness: the cultivation of a quality of attention that refuses to let the familiar become invisible. To practice, as the poet Mary Oliver put it, paying attention as though your life depended on it. Because in the ways that matter most, it does.

The Paradox at the Center

Here is the final paradox: we cannot live without normalization, and we cannot live well by surrendering to it completely. The capacity to habituate is what makes continuous existence tolerable. The capacity to un-habituate — to look again, to question the given, to feel the strangeness of the familiar — is what makes existence meaningful.

Normal is not a destination. It is a process — continuous, political, ethical, and personal. The question is not whether we normalize but what we normalize, how quickly, and whether we remain capable of waking up when we most need to.

The moment when a new normal truly becomes the normal is the moment we stop asking whether it should be. The work of a conscious life — and a conscious civilization — is to keep asking, even when the question feels unnecessary. Especially when it feels unnecessary.

The most radical act available to any of us, at any moment, is to look at what everyone has stopped looking at — and to ask, with genuine openness: is this how it has to be? Often the answer will be yes. But the asking is what keeps the yes honest, and what keeps the no possible.

— End of Essay
